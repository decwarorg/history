Sorry for the delayed response, but I’ve been following your email with Rich closely.  And I’ve also dug into decwar.org.  Fantastic site!!

Just for context, I was at UT starting as a grad student in 1971 and left in 1985 to join MCC.  I was on the Computation Center staff starting in 1975 as a systems programmer for the DECSystem-10 through 1982 when the ’10 was decommissioned.  The final party was the subject of “The Soul of an Old Machine”.  Then I became the manager of the Research-20, a DECSYSTEM-20, until I left in 1985.

Bob Hysick was also on the DEC-10 staff and I was well familiar with his DECWAR project.  One of my projects during those years was the development of TECO-124, which among many other things added video support for dumb terminals to DEC’s standard text editors, TECO.  I was happy to see the TECO macro directory in the DECWAR sources!

I was also friends with Dave Matuszek and Paul Reynolds, fellow grad students and the authors of the original STARTREK game on the CDC-6600, which was one of the main inspirations of DECWAR.  My friend and fellow grad student Rich Cohen was also a good friend of theirs.  I’ve shared your original email message with him.  He stays in touch with Dave and Paul and can give you their contact info.  He can also suggest a few corrections to your timeline.  For example, he tells me that “Super Star Trek” was not done at UT, and was based on a stolen copy of the Matuszek/Reynolds original FORTRAN source.

As some of you know, I am interested in preserving the History of Computing at UT as a collection within the University Archives. This relies and builds on the AMAZING and ESSENTIAL documentary efforts of many members of the IT community including: Chuck McClenon, Curtis Pew, Cheryl Fuller, Bill Wise, and Wallace Reeves - just to name a few.

The History of IT_at_UT wiki, individuals that have taken on the role of historian for their departments and groups that have generously sat down with me to share moments and observations from their time as members of the UT IT community > these people are providing the context that will allow present and future researchers (including faculty, students and staff of the university) to understand events in our computing history that have shaped the university's trajectory/history more broadly. 

Understanding our rationales and behaviors regarding the selection, adoption, creation, use, and modification of technology overtime is one way we can better understand ourselves: the ways in which technology mediates relationships between individuals, individuals and their organizations and between organizations/communities - just to name a few.

The challenge of this effort has not been finding people that are willing to share, but identifying ways to accomplish this documentation/collection systematically, and in earnest leading up to mainframe retirement.

Thankfully, computing history (and its many facets) is receiving more and more attention from scholarly researchers in fields ranging from information studies to architecture to linguistics to fine arts and english - just to name a few.

Working with researchers on and off campus with an interest in computing history is one way we can draw more attention and focus to, and make progress on this important documentary effort. Becky's research statement:

HI! I am a postdoctoral research associate at King’s College London in the English Faculty. I did my PhD at Oxford (2014) on the literary and cultural history of the interview form and practice. My current project is looking at how authors have thought about the reading/writing interface since the advent of digital computer - to do that I am focusing particularly on communication and the trope of conversation (whether involving humans or machines). 

My first case study is J. M. Coetzee, who won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 2003. He was born in South Africa, taking a degree in Literature and Maths. He started a computing career in the 1960s in the UK working for IBM (1962-64, using a 1401 and the 7090) and then on the Atlas 2/Titan supercomputer project at Cambridge (1964-5). Coetzee was used to very high level computing (the Atlas 2/Titan was one of the first computers to employ timesharing for instance), working with both business and scientific computers, and in very low level machine languages and on multiprogramming operating systems. At the same time he was writing an MA in literature (and bad computer poetry!) In 1965 Coetzee left the UK, coming to Austin to pursue a PhD in English. His dissertation looked at stylostatistics, and he used computers to analyse the prose style of Samuel Beckett’s Watt. He then went on to teach literature at college level, do research into issues around computing, linguistics and literature, and also writing computer poetry for the next 50 years, alongside writing novels.

I have been at the Harry Ransom Center as a fellow for the last month looking at Coetzee’s archival papers, but I am trying to establish what the computing culture was like at UT Austin at the time he was here (Aug 1965-Aug 1968) and what computers/programming languages he would have been using. Frustratingly, he kept all of his notes except for those associated with his PhD dissertation! I have had great help from Jim Browne and Nell Dale and have surmised the following:

Coetzee was taught the history of English and Old English by Winfred Lehmann in the English faculty. Lehmann was also Director of the Linguistics Research Center which was involved in computer analysis from 1963(?). This, I suspect, is the main link between Coetzee and computing at UT but I don’t have any proof.

I know the computation center was established in 1959 and the Computer Science dept in 1966, a year after he arrived. I know CS and the Linguistics Research Center were both very interdisciplinary, which given his various interests, would probably have been appealing to him. I don’t know which computers he would have been using (the CDC3600?, the Computer Science Dept’s CDC6600, purchased in 1966?) or what languages and software he used/wrote in order to do alphabetic analysis - SPSS was released in 1968 but he was more than capable of writing his own.

I am also exploring the possibility that Coetzee knew of the Machine Language experiments of Margaret Masterman, Roger Needham and others in Cambridge in the early 1960s while he was working there - he seemed interested in using Roget’s Thesaurus as a means for writing computer poetry, which is what Margaret Masterman had been promoting as a basis for semantic machine translation -  and followed up this interest at UT Austin (Robert Simmons arrived in 1968 from RAND to work on NLP and semantics). But I haven’t found anything in his notes yet… 

I am basically looking for any information about what Coetzee might have been doing with computers - officially or not! - at UT Austin and who he might have encountered. It’s rather broad!

Any tips, suggestions or documents would be very gratefully received!

The building is now mostly office space, although there may be a few servers still operating in the machine room there.
