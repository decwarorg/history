The IMP arrived on the UT campus in 1977 and was installed alongside the DEC-10 in the HRC. This was because originally there were hopes that the DEC-10 would become the first ARPANET host on the Texas node. But DEC never offered support for the ARPANET on the DEC-10’s standard OS, TOPS-10. The only DEC-10 OS's that supported a connection were TENEX (developed at BBN), ITS (the Incompatible Timesharing System at MIT), and several heavily customized versions of TOPS-10 at places like CMU, Harvard, and Wharton. The Stanford AI Lab ran WAITS (the West-coast Alternative to ITS) on their DEC-10s. So the DEC-10 in HRC was never connected to the ARPANET. Meanwhile, there was a PDP-11 running UNIX in the Daily Texan composing room at TSP (Texas Student Publications). A hacked version of UNIX from the U. of Illinois was installed, and the IMP was moved across campus to the Communications Building. Thus, a PDP-11/45 running UNIX became UTEXAS, the first ARPANET host on campus. The select few who knew about this would connect to the 11 via dialup modems and from there could venture out to the rest of the ARPANET.

Sometime around 1978-80, a newer IMP was delivered by BBN and installed in the Computation Center. A PDP-11/70 was installed there to connect to the new IMP, and so the UTEXAS host was rehomed from TSP to the Comp. Center. It was only when the first DEC-20 arrived and was installed in Painter Hall that the PDP-11 became UTEXAS-11 and the 20 was named UTEXAS-20. As I recall, the 11 retained the host nickname of UTEXAS.

At about the same time, specialized research hardware was acquired by the Certifiable Minicomputer Project (CMP) for the purpose of providing secure encrypted communications. This required that a very thick cable with 32 twisted pairs of copper be run from the 21st floor of the tower, down the elevator shaft and through the steam tunnels to the Comp. Center, where it plugged into the IMP. When the CMP project was completed several years later and the hardware decommissioned, the cable was left in the elevator shaft, as it was not worth the trouble to remove it. I wonder if the folks currently remodeling the Tower have come across it?! (Hat tip to Rich Cohen and Clyde Hoover for filling in some details of this story.)

Rich, the person you are thinking about across the hall at the Linguistics Research Center (LRC) was Dr. Helen Jo Hewitt, aka “HJ”.  She was a BIG fan of TECO, and was one of my “power” users.  She was one of the early “font hackers”, and I had developed a bunch of TECO macros for her.  She would come across the hall and pose an interesting problem, and a few minutes, or hours, or days(!) later, I would present her with another TECO macro for her growing collection. These tools would enable her, for example, to use a meta-language of her own creation to insert diacritics into a document, and then apply the appropriate macro to convert the meta-language into actual escape-sequence commands for the robotic typewriter to backspace, make micro-adjustments to the carriage, and type the appropriate symbol(s) to produce the desired diacritic at j-u-s-t the right position.
Later on, as her daisy-wheel collection of different type-fonts grew, a macro would be used to type a partially-filled page of text, then command the typewriter to roll the page back to the beginning and pause. She could then switch in, say, the Greek daisy-wheel, after which the job would continue with the typewriter skipping directly to each blank spot where a Greek character needed to be typed.

HJ and I stayed friends for years after she left UT until her death in 2017.  It helped that she was also a night owl!

I will insert a brief anecdote about the printer room.  Because the main machine room was on a raised floor, you had to step down about 6 inches to go into any of the offices from the machine room.  But when they installed the raised floor, they did not increase the height of any of the doors.  Normally this was not a problem, since those doors were still about 78-80 inches high, so just about everybody (including me at 6’ 4”) could go through without stooping.  Except for one Saturday night.  I was always a night owl, and you could usually find me there in the wee hours of the morning working at my terminal.   On this particular occasion I was making some extensive changes to TECO 124.  We didn’t have WYSIWYG screen editors then, so every 30 minutes or so I would print out the file to get fresh copy to continue editing.  I would have to leave my office and walk across the machine room to go through the door of the printer room to retrieve my printout.  I guess I got a bit too excited after one particular edit, and I remember enthusiastically bounding across the machine room toward the printer room door in a rush to get the printout.  The next thing I knew I was lying on my back, having crashed into the top of the doorway.  I had blood spewing from a gash on my forehead and a sharp pain in my lower back where I had landed on the step!  Since there was nobody around to help me, I ended up phoning the UT Police who transported me to the University Health Center up on 26th Street to get patched up.  The most interesting part of the story is that unbeknownst to me, the Heath Center reported my accident to the UT Safety Office.  On Monday morning they sent somebody to inspect the doorways into the DEC-10 machine room.  They declared it a safety violation, and in less than two weeks had modified all the doors/doorways by extending their height by 6 inches!

I was sad to hear about Robert Schneider’s death.   The T-shirt he was wearing was made especially for the DEC-10 going-away party in 1982.  The decision to shut down the -10 was hotly protested by staff and users alike.  It ended up going all the way up the UT Vice President for Research, Dr. Gerhard Fonken, who made the final decision.  Many, including Robert, had the view that the move had been made by a clueless UT executive who probably didn’t have any familiarity with the DEC-10, and that if he had played DECWAR even once, he might have changed his mind.  Wishful thinking…!
